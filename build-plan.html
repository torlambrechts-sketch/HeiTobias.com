Phase 0 — the core the whole system sits on

Before any feature ships, the platform needs its primitives: the entities everything else references (organization, people, teams, roles) and the plumbing that makes it multi-tenant, secure and connected (rights, integrations, settings, audit). This isn't glamorous, but get it wrong and every later module inherits the pain — especially the three-party data model and EU residency, which are near-impossible to retrofit. Build this first.

ACore entities — the data model
Organization / Tenant
Top-level boundary. Every record belongs to a tenant; data is isolated per org. Supports the multi-tenant split between agency orgs and employer orgs that the hand-off depends on.
root of all RLS · agency ⇄ employer types
People (Person record)
The universal person entity — a candidate, an employee, a manager, a recruiter are all People in different states. One identity that persists from candidate → hire → tenure, carrying the profile.
candidate · employee · manager · recruiter
Teams & Departments
Hierarchical org structure — departments contain teams contain people. The unit the team composition engine and team-based role definition operate on; also how reporting lines and manager scope are derived.
nested · reporting lines · manager scope
Roles (the role profile)
Entity A of the spine — a first-class record, not a job-title string. Versioned, team-authored, attached to positions. A position is an instance of a role a specific person fills.
role profile ≠ position ≠ job title
Profiles & Assessments
Entity B — the person's cognitive/personality/values data, plus the assessment instances that produced it. Linked to the Person, consent-scoped, time-stamped for re-fit history.
consent-scoped · versioned over tenure
Requisitions & Placements
The hiring transaction object — a requisition links a role + a team + candidates; a placement is the closed event that triggers the consent-gated hand-off from agency to employer.
the hand-off trigger · links agency↔employer
BAccess, rights & security
Identity & SSO
Login via Okta, Entra ID, Google Workspace. SCIM provisioning so employees/managers sync automatically from the company directory. No manual user management at scale.
SSO · SCIM · MFA
RBAC — roles & rights
Granular permission roles: recruiter, hiring manager, people-ops admin, manager, employee, org admin. What each can see and do is scoped by role and by their position in the org tree.
role × scope · least-privilege default
Row-level security
Enforced at the database layer (Supabase RLS), not just the UI. A manager sees only their reports; an agency sees only its requisitions. Security that can't be bypassed by a crafted request.
Postgres RLS · tenant + scope isolation
Consent & data ownership
The consent ledger — who consented to what, when, revocable. Powers the candidate-owned portable profile and the three-party (agency·candidate·employer) hand-off. GDPR core, not an add-on.
consent ledger · purpose limitation · revocable
CIntegrations, settings & operations
HRIS & ATS integrations
Two-way sync with HiBob, Personio, Workday (HRIS) and the agency's ATS. Don't reinvent the system of record — read org/people structure in, write profiles/outcomes back.
HiBob · Personio · Workday · ATS connectors
Workflow integrations
Slack, Teams, calendar, email — the channels guidance and prompts are delivered through. Webhooks & an open API so customers and partners can extend.
Slack · Teams · calendar · webhooks · API
Org settings & configuration
Per-tenant config: branding, languages (NO/SE/DK/EN), assessment defaults, re-fit cadence, notification rules, data-retention policy. The modular config pattern from your Conscia work.
branding · locale · cadences · retention
Audit log & data residency
Immutable, queryable audit trail of every consequential action. EU-region hosting and residency guarantees. The substrate the EU AI Act technical documentation is generated from.
immutable log · EU region · AI-Act-ready
Billing & plans
Per-seat (agency), per-hire (activation), per-employee (lifecycle) — the three pricing layers metered separately. Usage tracking, seats, subscription state.
per-seat · per-hire · per-employee metering
Notifications & jobs
The scheduler & event system behind re-fit reminders, pulse cadences, onboarding milestones and digest delivery. Background jobs that keep the lifecycle running without manual triggers.
scheduler · event bus · background jobs
Why Phase 0 is non-negotiable Three things here cannot be retrofitted without a painful rebuild: multi-tenancy (agency vs. employer isolation), the consent ledger / three-party data model, and EU data residency. The rest of the platform assumes these exist. Build the core thin but correct — you don't need every integration on day one, but you do need the entity model, RLS, consent and tenancy right from the first commit.
Feature spec — module by module
R
Role Architecture Engine
the measuring stick · entity A of the spine
Phase 1

The most overlooked entity in the category. Every fit-score, prediction and piece of guidance is a comparison — and a comparison needs a defined target. This engine builds, versions and evolves the role profile. In v1 it ships as a library of pre-built, advisor-validated role templates that teams tune — not blank job-analysis (which would produce garbage-in-garbage-out).

P1Template library
20–30 common roles pre-built and validated by an I/O advisor. Teams start from a sane default rather than a blank form. Expands over time into a versioned catalogue.
P1Weighted competencies
Behavioural & skill dimensions that predict success, each importance-weighted — not a flat checklist. The weights drive the fit score.
P1Trait target ranges
Optimal bands per trait, not "more is better." Too much conscientiousness in a fast-pivot role is as much a misfit as too little. More correct and more defensible.
P1Context factors
Autonomy, ambiguity tolerance, pace, collaboration intensity, stakeholder load. The situational demands that traits get scored against.
P3Evolution vector
Where the role is heading over the next 12–24 months. The conceptual bridge that connects hiring to development — you grow people toward where the role is going.
P1Versioning & sign-off
Every change is versioned and attributable. "The role was defined like this, by these people, on this date" — your audit trail and legal armor.
Why it mattersDefining the role rigorously is what makes "fit" mean anything — and what makes a hiring decision defensible under the EU AI Act, where requirements must be demonstrably job-relevant.
stack: role library · versioned schema · template engine · Supabase/Postgres
T
Team-Based Role Definition
collective authoring · bias reduction · team-fit
Phase 1

A role defined by one manager is a single point of bias — usually "someone like me" or "someone who fixes my current pain." When the team that will work with the role co-defines it, you get a fuller picture, built-in bias reduction, and a documented collective decision. The hard part — and where the product wins — is the reconciliation flow.

P1Independent rating
Each team member rates criteria importance separately, before seeing others — classic structured-decision technique that prevents anchoring on the loudest voice.
P1Divergence surfacing
The system highlights disagreement rather than averaging it away. "Manager weights autonomy high; team weights collaboration high" — that conflict is the most useful signal in the room.
P1Reconciliation session
A guided flow to resolve divergence into a final weighted profile. Forces a sharper, shared definition of the role before anyone is assessed against it.
P3Team composition gap
Reads the existing team's collective profile (from members' own validated assessments) to identify what's missing. The candidate is scored vs. role + team gap, not role alone.
P1Quick vs. workshop paths
A fast "template tune" path for high-volume roles, and a full team-workshop path for senior/critical hires. Friction matched to stakes.
P1Documented decision
Collective, attributable definition = strong bias-reduction evidence. Far more defensible than "the manager decided."
Critical guardrailTeam composition is built from each member's own validated profile — NOT peers rating each other's personalities. The former is powerful and defensible; the latter is surveillance that poisons trust and fails bias scrutiny. Build the former only.
stack: multi-evaluator flow · divergence engine · aggregation service · RBAC scoping
A
Assessment Module
entity B seed · cognitive + personality + work-style
Phase 1

Creates the Person Profile — the seed of the entire data spine. Deliberately positioned as a finalist deep-dive, not a top-of-funnel screener: the 2–3 candidates who matter get a rich, ~30-minute assessment whose data lives for the employee's whole tenure. In v1, license validated instruments rather than building from scratch.

P1Cognitive ability
Adaptive ~15–20 min test, calibrated to the role's cognitive demand level. The strongest single predictor of performance after structured interviews.
P1Personality / work-style
Big Five-based (e.g. IPIP-NEO licensed for v1), ~8–10 min. Scored against the role's trait target ranges, not generic norms.
P1Values & drivers
Motivational fit — what energises this person. Feeds both the hiring decision and later the guidance & growth layers.
P1Validity & consistency checks
Response-consistency, social-desirability and careless-responding flags so a profile is trustworthy before it seeds the spine.
P1Candidate experience
Mobile-first, no-login, branded for the agency or employer. Low abandonment — assessment UX is a real differentiator.
P4Proprietary instruments
Replace licensed tests with Nordic-normed proprietary instruments once enough data exists — part of the long-term moat.
Hard dependencyThis module requires a credentialed I/O psychologist on the team or as a public advisor. Without one, the "science-backed" claim is hollow and collapses at the first sophisticated procurement question.
stack: adaptive item engine · scoring service · edge functions · licensed instrument layer
F
Fit Scoring & Placement Report
the comparison · the recruiter's deliverable
Phase 1

Where the two entities meet. Person measured against role (and team gap) produces a fit picture — and for the recruiter, a client-ready placement report that signals rigor and justifies their fee. This is the deliverable that makes the standalone Phase-1 product sellable on its own.

P1Multi-dimensional fit
Not one number. Fit shown per competency, trait-range, cognitive demand and context factor — so a manager sees where someone fits and where they'll stretch.
P1Strengths & friction
Predictable strengths and predictable friction points for this person in this specific role — actionable, not abstract trait labels.
P1Placement report (client-ready)
Branded, defensible summary the agency hands its client. Turns "trust me" into "here's the structured evidence" — the recruiter's value-signal.
P1Side-by-side shortlist
Compare finalists against the same role target. Structured comparison instead of gut-feel ranking.
P3Quality floors
Guardrails that prevent a low-rigor profile from being handed over — protects the inheriting employer (and your reputation) from garbage-in.
P4Adverse-impact view
Surfaces potential disparate impact in selection — the precursor to full bias-audit automation in Phase 4.
FramingThe fit score informs a human decision — it never makes it. Human-in-the-loop isn't just good practice; it's an EU AI Act requirement for high-risk hiring systems.
stack: scoring service · report generator (PDF/web) · comparison engine
H
The Hand-off & Portability
the conversion mechanism · land-and-expand
Phase 2

The strategic heart of the channel play. At placement, the role + person profile travels to the employer, who arrives pre-seeded instead of blank. This is what turns the agency into a customer-acquisition engine and creates a real switching cost — the data is already there.

P2Candidate-owned profile
The profile belongs to the candidate, who consents to it traveling. "Your profile is yours and follows you" is a genuine selling point to candidates, not just compliance.
P2Consent-gated transfer
Explicit, purpose-limited consent for the profile to move to the employer and be used post-hire. Logged, revocable, GDPR-clean.
P2Model 1 — hand-over
Separate agency & employer instances; profile transfers at placement. How agencies bring you employers who aren't customers yet — the acquisition path.
P2Model 2 — shared workspace
Employer already on-platform invites the agency into a requisition as a scoped collaborator. Data is simply already there at close. Stickier.
P2Employer onboarding
First-run experience that activates the inherited data immediately — no re-entry, instant value, low activation friction.
P2Channel posture controls
Clear rules on what the agency sees after hand-off vs. the employer — so agencies trust the platform and don't fear disintermediation.
Design-in from schemaThis is a three-party GDPR relationship (agency · candidate · employer). Consent, purpose limitation and portability must be in the data model from day one — retrofitting later is painful. Your security background should own this early.
stack: multi-tenant model · consent ledger · scoped data transfer · SSO/SCIM
90
The 90-Day Kickstart
first proof of the lifecycle value
Phase 2

The first taste of life beyond hiring — and the feature that proves the post-hire layer is worth paying for. The inherited profile auto-generates a tailored onboarding plan for the manager: not "schedule a 1:1," but specific actions for this person given how they work.

P2Profile-tailored plan
A structured 90-day journey generated from the new hire's traits, values and the role's context. Specific, dated, actionable — delivered to the manager weekly.
P2Manager prompts
"This person has low ambiguity tolerance — front-load structure in week one; here are 3 concrete moves." Turns profile data into Monday-morning action.
P2Milestone tracking
30/60/90 checkpoints with light structured check-ins — early signal on whether the onboarding is landing.
P3Workflow delivery
Pushes prompts into Slack/Teams/calendar so guidance reaches the manager where they work, not in a portal they forget to open.
Why hereThe 90-day plan is the bridge: it's delivered at hire (so it converts the placement into an active employer account) but it's a lifecycle feature (so it proves the Phase-3 layer is worth buying).
stack: lifecycle orchestrator · guidance composer · notification hub
M
Manager Workspace & Guidance Composer
the retention engine · the daily surface
Phase 3

The core of the lifecycle business and the surface that separates you from every transactional assessment tool. Most platforms produce PDFs for HR that managers never read. This makes the manager the user and delivers specific, continuous guidance about the people they actually manage.

P3Living per-report profile
For each direct report: how they work, what motivates them, where they struggle, what to do this week. Updated by ongoing pulse data, not frozen at hire.
P31:1 preparation
"Today's 1:1 with Erik — he tends to under-report stress; ask these 4 questions." Concrete prep, generated from profile + recent signals.
P3Guidance composer
Turns profile + role + tenure + framework into natural, specific advice. Grounded in the frameworks library via RAG — never freeform. Freeform advice about people is a liability.
P3Frameworks library
The management & development IP behind the guidance — playbooks mapped to traits and contexts. This is real content work, not just software.
P3Pulse & signals
Lightweight, frequent check-ins that keep profiles live — replacing the once-a-year engagement survey with a continuous, low-burden signal.
P3Workflow-native delivery
Guidance arrives in Slack/Teams/calendar/email digest. A tool managers don't have to remember to open is a tool they actually use.
Culture-design cautionThis is where your management experience matters most. The same data that reads as "developmental support" can read as "surveillance." Employee consent, ownership and developmental framing determine whether people engage honestly or game it.
stack: manager web app · guidance composer (LLM + RAG) · frameworks content engine · pulse events
C
Team Composition Engine
what founders & team leads actually want
Phase 3

Aggregates individual profiles into a team-level view: collective strengths, predictable friction, and fit for a given project or challenge. This is the feature scale-up leaders intuitively want — "do we have the right mix for what's coming?"

P3Composition map
The team's combined strengths and gaps at a glance — built from members' own validated profiles, never peer-ratings.
P3Predictable friction
"This team will struggle with the upcoming change initiative — here's a 2-week protocol." Anticipates team dynamics before they bite.
P3Project-fit view
Match team composition to a specific project's demands; flag what's missing and who to add or borrow.
P3Hiring-gap feed
Feeds the team-gap context back into the Role Architecture engine — closing the loop between "who we have" and "who we hire next."
stack: aggregation service · team-graph model · composition visualiser
↻
Re-Fit & Growth Engine
fit as a trajectory, not a snapshot
Phase 3

Both the person and the role drift over time. Periodically re-measuring the person against the evolved role produces a fit trajectory — and the gap between person-drift and role-drift is the most actionable signal in the system. Catching divergence early is a board-level retention metric.

P3Periodic re-evaluation
Re-measure on a cadence against the current role version. Consent-owned and developmentally framed — a growth check-in, never a verdict.
P3Four-quadrant signal
Growth gap (develop) · flight risk (promote) · stable fit (leave alone) · emerging misfit (intervene early). The last one is the retention gold.
P3Growth conversations
Surfaces the right development conversation per person, per tenure, per role-direction — replacing generic OKR templates with specific human dialogue.
P4Outcome capture
Logs actual performance outcomes — the ground truth that closes the prediction loop and feeds the Phase-4 models.
Framing is everything"You're falling behind" kills trust; "here's where the role is heading and how to grow toward it" builds it. Same data, opposite outcomes. This is a culture-design problem wearing a data costume.
stack: re-assessment scheduler · drift analytics · growth-conversation engine
∑
Predictive Intelligence & Bias Audit
the compounding moat · EU AI Act layer
Phase 4

Where the data advantage compounds into something competitors can't catch. Every placement and re-fit produces a Person × Role × Outcome triple. Over time these train predictions nobody storing only people, or only static roles, can assemble. This phase also automates the EU AI Act obligations that hiring-AI triggers.

P4Performance prediction
Role-fit and performance models trained on accumulated outcome data — the long-term moat, legitimate because it's grounded in real outcomes, not theory.
P4Bias & fairness audit
Adverse-impact monitoring across protected groups, automated. Mandatory under the EU AI Act — hiring is classified high-risk. A feature, not a bolt-on.
P4AI Act documentation
Auto-generates the technical documentation, human-oversight evidence and audit trail regulators require — turning compliance into a selling point.
P4Nordic validation set
Locally-normed data and proprietary instruments — the cultural-fit defensibility vs. US-built incumbents (Plum, Predictive Index, Cangrade).
The moat, stated plainlyA competitor can copy any single feature. They cannot copy the accumulated Person × Role × Outcome dataset that gets sharper with every hire. The role profile being first-class is half of why this data is uniquely yours.
stack: model registry · ML pipeline · fairness-metric engine · research data store · EU region
The motion & how it's priced

Land with the recruiter. Expand into the employer.

Phase 1–2 is a standalone recruiter product — sold to Nordic agencies on placement-quality and defensibility, priced per-seat. It seeds profiles. Phase 3 flips on the lifecycle layer inside the employers those agencies brought in — priced per-employee, 3–5× the ACV, with net revenue retention above 110% because the data's already there and the switching cost is real. Same spine, two revenue layers, land-and-expand built into the structure.

Acquisition layer
Recruiter — per seat
Agencies pay per recruiter seat for role definition + assessment + placement reports. Fast sales cycle, clear ROI (better placements, fewer re-dos under guarantee).
Activation layer
Employer — per hire
Inherited profile + 90-day kickstart converts the placement into an active employer account. Low friction — the value is visible on day one.
Expansion layer
Lifecycle — per employee
Manager guidance, team composition, re-fit. The high-margin, high-retention revenue. This is the real business; recruiting is how you get here.
Two honest constraints This is a raise-capital, build-a-team, multi-year business — not an 8-week bootstrap. And it has a hard dependency on an I/O psychologist and a part-time legal/AI-Act advisor before the first contract. The phasing de-risks it: each phase is sellable alone, so you validate demand and revenue before committing to the next. If Phase 1 (the recruiter product) doesn't sell, you learn that cheaply — before building the expensive lifecycle and intelligence layers.
